Complex Analysis: Holomorphic Functions & Beyond

Core Definition: Holomorphic Functions

A complex function \(f: \Omega \to \mathbb{C}\) is holomorphic on an open subset \(\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}\) if it is complex differentiable at every point in \(\Omega\).

$$f'(z_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0)}{h}$$

This limit must exist and be the same regardless of the direction from which \(h\) approaches zero in the complex plane.

The Cauchy-Riemann Equations

For a complex function \(f(z) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y)\) where \(z = x + iy\), the function is holomorphic if and only if it satisfies the Cauchy-Riemann equations:

$$\boxed{\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}}$$

Why These Equations?

Approaching the derivative along the real axis:

$$f'(z_0) = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + i\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}$$

From the Imaginary Axis

Approaching along the imaginary axis:

$$f'(z_0) = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} - i\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}$$

Example: \(f(z) = z^2\)

Let's verify that \(f(z) = z^2 = (x+iy)^2 = x^2 - y^2 + 2ixy\) is holomorphic:

  • â€¢ \(u(x,y) = x^2 - y^2\), \(v(x,y) = 2xy\)
  • â€¢ \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = 2x\), \(\frac{\partial v}{\partial y} = 2x\) âœ“
  • â€¢ \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -2y\), \(-\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} = -2y\) âœ“

Euler's Formula & Geometric Interpretation

Euler's Identity

$$e^{i\theta} = \cos(\theta) + i\sin(\theta)$$

This formula reveals that multiplication by \(e^{i\theta}\) represents a rotation by angle \(\theta\) in the complex plane.

The connection to holomorphic functions: Complex differentiability requires that locally, the function acts as a scaled rotation. If \(f'(z_0) = re^{i\phi}\), then near \(z_0\):

$$f(z) \approx f(z_0) + re^{i\phi}(z - z_0)$$

Remarkable Properties of Holomorphic Functions

ðŸ”„ Infinite Differentiability

If \(f\) is holomorphic once in a region, it's automatically infinitely differentiable there!

$$f^{(n)}(z) \text{ exists } \forall n \in \mathbb{N}$$

ðŸŽ¯ Analytic Representation

Holomorphic functions can be represented by convergent power series:

$$f(z) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n(z-z_0)^n$$

ðŸŒŠ Conformal Mapping

Holomorphic functions preserve angles and infinitesimally preserve shapes (where \(f'(z) \neq 0\)).

âš¡ Maximum Principle

A non-constant holomorphic function cannot attain its maximum inside its domain.

Neural Phasor Networks Connection

Modern Application: Neural phasor networks leverage complex-valued computations where activations exist in the complex plane. The Wirtinger calculus enables backpropagation through non-holomorphic activation functions.

Wirtinger Derivatives

For non-holomorphic functions in neural networks, we use:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial z} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} - i\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right)$$ $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial z^*} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + i\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right)$$

Complex Neural ODE Formulation

In the context of Neural ODEs with complex values:

$$\frac{dz(t)}{dt} = f_\theta(z(t), z^*(t), t)$$

This formulation allows modeling of continuous-time dynamics in complex phase space, potentially capturing oscillatory and periodic behaviors naturally.

Activation Functions for Complex Networks

  • CReLU: \(f(z) = \text{ReLU}(\text{Re}(z)) + i \cdot \text{ReLU}(\text{Im}(z))\)
  • ModReLU: \(f(z) = \text{ReLU}(|z| + b) \cdot \frac{z}{|z|}\) (preserves phase)
  • Complex Tanh: \(f(z) = \tanh(z)\) (fully complex processing)

Novel Holomorphic Activation Functions

ðŸŒŸ Holomorphic Complex ELU

A fully holomorphic variant of the Exponential Linear Unit that maintains complex differentiability everywhere:

$$f_{\mathrm{H{\text -}ELU}}(z) = \begin{cases} z & \text{if } \mathrm{Re}(z) > 0 \\ \alpha(e^z - 1) & \text{if } \mathrm{Re}(z) \leq 0 \end{cases}$$

For complex \(z = x + iy\):

  • â€¢ Positive real part: Identity mapping preserves both magnitude and phase
  • â€¢ Negative real part: Smooth exponential transition with \(\alpha(e^{x+iy} - 1) = \alpha e^x(\cos y + i\sin y) - \alpha\)
  • â€¢ Key property: Unlike ModReLU, this doesn't break holomorphicity by taking absolute values
  • â€¢ Gradient flow: \(f'(z) = 1\) for \(\mathrm{Re}(z) > 0\), and \(f'(z) = \alpha e^z\) for \(\mathrm{Re}(z) \leq 0\)
Implementation insight: The condition on \(\mathrm{Re}(z)\) only (not \(|z|\)) preserves the holomorphic structure while providing similar gating behavior to ModReLU.

ðŸŽ¯ Holomorphic Complex Swish

A smooth, self-gated activation function that remains holomorphic throughout the complex plane:

$$f_{\mathrm{H{\text -}Swish}}(z) = z \cdot \sigma(\beta z) = \frac{z}{1 + e^{-\beta z}}$$

where \(\sigma\) is the complex sigmoid function.

Mathematical Properties

The complex sigmoid extends naturally:

$$\sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}} = \frac{e^z}{e^z + 1}$$

This is holomorphic everywhere except at the poles where \(e^z = -1\)

Gradient Computation

The derivative maintains elegant form:

$$f'(z) = \sigma(\beta z) + \beta z \sigma(\beta z)(1 - \sigma(\beta z))$$

Smooth gradient flow without vanishing/exploding issues

Advantages over ModReLU:
  • â€¢ Fully differentiable with no kinks or discontinuities
  • â€¢ Self-gating mechanism naturally modulates both magnitude and phase
  • â€¢ Parameter \(\beta\) controls the sharpness of the gating transition
  • â€¢ No explicit magnitude computation (no \(|z|\)) preserves holomorphicity

Complex Neural ODEs & Fourier Neural ODEs

ðŸŒŠ Complex Neural ODEs

Complex Neural ODEs extend the standard NODE framework to complex-valued state spaces, enabling natural modeling of oscillatory and wave-like phenomena:

$$\frac{dz(t)}{dt} = f_\theta(z(t), z^*(t), t), \quad z(0) = z_0 \in \mathbb{C}^n$$

Where \(f_\theta\) maps from \(\mathbb{C}^n \times \mathbb{C}^n \times \mathbb{R}\) to \(\mathbb{C}^n\) and is parameterized by a complex neural network.

Key Advantages

  • â€¢ Natural representation of periodic/oscillatory dynamics
  • â€¢ Phase and magnitude evolve jointly
  • â€¢ Parameter efficiency (up to 224x reduction)
  • â€¢ Preserves geometric structure in phase space

Implementation in JAX

Holomorphic Integration: When using holomorphic activation functions (H-ELU, H-Swish), the ODE preserves complex differentiability throughout the evolution, potentially enabling more stable long-term integration.

ðŸ”„ Fourier Neural ODEs (FNODEs)

FNODEs leverage Fourier analysis for gradient estimation, enabling simulation-free training by directly matching vector fields in frequency space:

$$\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \left\| \hat{s}^{\prime}(t_n) - f_\theta[s(t_n), u(t_n), \nabla_x s(t_n), \ldots] \right\|$$

Where the temporal gradient is estimated via Fourier transform:

$$\hat{s}^{\prime}(t_n) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=0}^{K} i\frac{2\pi k}{T} \tilde{s}_k e^{i\frac{2\pi k}{N}n}$$

Key Innovations

Gradient Flow Matching

Directly match temporal gradients estimated from Fourier analysis, avoiding expensive ODE solves during training:

$$\tilde{s}_k = \mathcal{F}(s)_k = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} s_n e^{-i\frac{2\pi nk}{N}}$$
Spatial Gradient Estimation

For PDEs, estimate spatial derivatives via Fourier methods:

$$\frac{\partial^{p+q} s}{\partial x^p \partial y^q} = \mathcal{F}^{-1}[(ik_x)^p(ik_y)^q \mathcal{F}(s)]$$
Data Augmentation Loop

Generate synthetic data via forward integration, then retrain with higher frequency resolution:

Train â†’ Generate â†’ Increase K â†’ Retrain

Performance Advantages

  • 10x+ faster training compared to standard NODEs (no backprop through ODE solver)
  • Superior noise robustness due to Fourier smoothing properties
  • Resolution-invariant modeling capabilities
  • Handles irregular sampling via NFFT (Nonuniform FFT)
